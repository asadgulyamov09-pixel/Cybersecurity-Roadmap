Policy Framework and RACI

Overview

Effective governance requires both clear documentation and clear accountability.

Policies define what must be done.

RACI defines who is responsible for doing it.

Together, they create structure and accountability within an organization.

---

Policy Framework

Organizations typically use a hierarchy of documents:

Policy
↓
Standard
↓
Procedure
↓
Guideline

---

Policy

A high-level statement that defines management expectations.

Example:

Information Security Policy

Purpose:

To protect organizational information and systems.

---

Standard

Mandatory requirements that support a policy.

Example:

Password Standard

Requirements:

- Minimum 12 characters
- MFA required
- Password reuse prohibited

---

Procedure

Step-by-step instructions.

Example:

How to Enable MFA

1. Open security settings.
2. Select MFA.
3. Configure authentication method.
4. Verify activation.

---

Guideline

Recommended practices.

Example:

Use a password manager for strong passwords.

Guidelines are not mandatory but help improve security.

---

RACI Model

RACI defines responsibilities for activities and controls.

Responsible (R)

Performs the work.

Example:

IT Team

---

Accountable (A)

Owns the result and final decision.

Example:

CIO

---

Consulted (C)

Provides expertise and advice.

Example:

Security Team

---

Informed (I)

Receives updates and status information.

Example:

CEO

---

Example: Access Control Policy

Role| Responsibility
IT Team| Responsible
CIO| Accountable
Security Team| Consulted
CEO| Informed

---

Why RACI Matters

Without RACI:

- Responsibilities become unclear
- Tasks may be duplicated
- Important activities may be missed
- Accountability is weakened

RACI ensures that governance processes operate effectively.

---

Main Lesson

Policy defines what must be done.

RACI defines who is responsible for making it happen.

Both are essential for effective governance and cybersecurity management.
